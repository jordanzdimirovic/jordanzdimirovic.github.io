Carbon Dioxide

The prevalence of CO2, a greenhouse gas emitted by countries across the globe.

Global Glance


Through the lens of a world-map, the modern contributors of carbon-dioxide can be visualised.
Global industrialisation, felt most strongly in China and particular western countries (most notably, the United States of America) is a likely driver of these numbers.

When rolling back the clock, a rapid increase in China's carbon emissions can be observed during the first decade of the century.

Tip: Use the slider to compare emissions across the last 100 years.

Monetary Motivations


It is reasonable to conclude that the economical health of a country may have predictive power over its emissions.
Comparing GDP per capita, a standard indicator for a country's wealth, and the annual carbon emissions of the country, a slight negative correlation is observed.
This indicates that wealthier countries are better placed to have lower emissions.

Note that the bubble size indicates the country's population.

Changing Climate


A decade-long comparison window was selected to obtain a perspective on change. Unfortunately, the results are not entirely positive.

Many western countries have made welcome strides against their carbon emissions; countries such as the United States of America and Japan with the most significant improvement over the ten-year period.

Discouragingly, the same cannot be said for India and China. These alarming increases over the last ten years should likely be considered a "pollusion proxy" from much of the developed world; much of the emissions are a direct result of the western world's delegation of production.
